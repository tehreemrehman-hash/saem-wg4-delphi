Modified Delphi Consensus Process

Welcome to WG4

This platform facilitates a modified Delphi consensus process to establish research priorities in Human-AI Interaction and the Perception of Self in emergency medicine. Through structured rounds of rating and discussion, our working group will identify high-priority research questions to advance the field.

Key Process Features:

  • Anonymous ratings preserve objectivity
  • Multiple rounds allow convergence toward consensus
  • Transparent feedback enables informed reconsideration
  • Open discussion surfaces rationale and alternatives

Participant Instructions

How to Participate

  1. Rate Questions: In Round 1, review each research question and select: "Include," "Include with Modifications," or "Exclude"
  2. Provide Feedback: Share specific suggestions for modifications in the comment box below each question
  3. Propose Questions: At the end of Round 1, suggest additional research questions if needed
  4. Participate in Discussion: Use the Discussion tab to elaborate on your rationale and engage with peers
  5. Review Results: In Round 2, you'll see aggregated Round 1 results and can refine your ratings based on group feedback
  6. Submit Once Per Round: Click "Submit Responses" when you've completed your ratings. You cannot change responses after submission.

Important Notes

  • Your responses are anonymous and confidential
  • Each rating is equally weighted regardless of expertise or experience
  • The goal is convergence toward group consensus, not unanimity
  • Respectful disagreement and diverse perspectives strengthen the process

Identified Research Gaps

  • Limited empirical work on clinician cognitive processes when using AI in real ED environments
  • Few rigorous interventions to mitigate automation bias in high-pressure clinical settings
  • Sparse literature on effects of AI on professional identity and meaning in medicine
  • Inadequate methods for evaluating human dimensions of AI (reasoning, wellbeing, patient communication)
  • Few studies examining long-term trust dynamics and skill atrophy with AI assistance
  • Limited guidance on governance and oversight of adaptive learning systems in clinical practice
  • Sparse research on patient perspectives and disclosure practices around AI in emergency care
