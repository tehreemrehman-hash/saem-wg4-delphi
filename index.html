Delphi Round 1: Rate Candidate Research Questions

What is Round 1? This is the first phase of our modified Delphi consensus process. You will review the candidate research questions across four domains and vote on each for inclusion in WG4's 10-year research agenda using the conference-wide 3-point Delphi scale.

How this question set was derived

The candidate questions below were drafted by the WG4 lead in April 2026 from three sources: (a) the WG4 evidence brief (~30 PubMed-indexed papers across 8 themes — see the Evidence Brief tab), (b) the SAEM 2026 Consensus Conference charter for Workgroup 4 (Human-AI Interaction and the Perception of Self), and (c) prior research-priority statements in clinical AI. They are explicitly framed as a seed list, not a closed canon. Round 1 includes a free-text field where panelists can propose additional questions; items proposed by ≥2 panelists will be added to the Round 2 slate as new items.

Reviewers who feel a topic is missing (e.g., ambient AI scribes, longitudinal effects) are specifically encouraged to use the additions field rather than forcing the concern into an "Include with modifications" vote on an unrelated question.

Rating scale & thresholds (conference-wide Delphi methodology)

For each question, select one of three options:

Include
Keep as written on WG4's agenda
Include w/ modifications
Keep in principle, but wording/scope needs revision
Exclude
Not appropriate for the WG4 agenda

Thresholds (applied to combined Include + Include-with-modifications percentage): ≥80% → confirmed, retired from voting · 21–79% → re-voted in Round 2 · ≤20% → removed. Two-round Delphi (compressed from the traditional three to fit the conference timeline, per the Working Group Leader Guide).

Each item also has a per-question comment box. Please use it whenever you vote "Include with modifications" (describe the modification) or "Exclude" (briefly explain why). Comments drive the Meeting 2 deliberation — they are not optional decoration.

Delphi Round 2: Review Results & Refine Ratings

What is Round 2? In this round, only the items that did not reach ≥80% inclusion in Round 1 (and were not ≤20% excluded) return for re-voting. You will see each re-voted item's Round 1 distribution (Include / Include-with-mods / Exclude percentages) and the representative comment themes from Round 1, then re-vote with that information in front of you. Items whose wording was revised by the WG4 leads after Round 1 will show a diff marker so you can see what changed; major rewrites return as new items.

What's different in Round 2

  • Same conference-wide 3-point scale (Include / Include with modifications / Exclude).
  • Confirmed items (≥80% Include in R1) and excluded items (≤20% Include in R1) are retired and not re-presented.
  • For each re-voted item, you will see: R1 % Include / % Modify / % Exclude bar chart, 3–5 representative comment quotes from Round 1, and a diff if the wording changed.
  • New items proposed by ≥2 panelists in the Round 1 "missing questions" field will appear at the end as new items for first-time voting.
  • Items still in the 21–79% pending band after Round 2 are resolved by co-lead adjudication at Meeting 3, informed by R1 + R2 comment threads — per the two-round Delphi specified in the SAEM 2026 Working Group Leader Guide.

Evidence Brief: State of the Science

What the evidence says (and doesn't) — 60-second read

If you only have a minute before Meeting 1, read this. It's the shape of the landscape the Q-slate is trying to cover.

Reasonably well established:

  • LLM-assisted clinicians can outperform solo clinicians on differential-diagnosis accuracy in controlled settings (McDuff 2025, Goh 2024).
  • Automation bias is real, measurable, and pulls readers toward incorrect calls when AI suggestions are visible (Dratsch 2023).
  • Ambient AI scribes reduce documentation time and after-hours work in ambulatory settings, with early burnout-score improvements in small pilots (Shah 2025, Ma 2025, Misurac 2024, Duggan 2025).

Contested or thin:

  • Whether current explainability/XAI methods actually improve clinical decisions, or mostly signal trustworthiness without delivering interpretability (Ghassemi 2021 vs. Chanda 2024).
  • Long-term effects on clinician skill — early deskilling signal from endoscopy after sustained AI exposure (Budzyń 2025), but no ED data.
  • The direction and mechanism of AI's effect on burnout — varies by implementation, specialty, and whether the clinician controls the tool.

Barely studied in the ED specifically:

  • Ambient AI scribes in the ED (Hassan 2025 and Duggan 2025 both explicitly flag this gap).
  • Cognitive load and automation bias under ED time pressure.
  • Effects on ED clinician professional identity, moral distress, and meaning.
  • Patient perception of AI involvement in emergency care and what disclosure should look like.

New in this refresh: Omar et al. 2025 (Nature Medicine) is the first large-scale study to document sociodemographic bias in LLM clinical decision making using 1,000 ED cases — cases labeled Black, unhoused, or LGBTQIA+ were disproportionately routed to invasive workups or mental-health evaluations. This is the strongest ED-specific evidence for the ethics/equity research agenda.

How this maps to the Q-slate: Sections 1–4 of this brief ground Domain A (Clinician-AI Interaction). Sections 5–6 ground Domain B (Professional Identity & Wellbeing). Section 7 grounds Domain C (Patient Experience). Section 8 now has the Omar 2025 anchor, but the moral-distress and clinician-accountability angles remain thin and are good targets for the brainstorm block.

Coming to Meeting 1: pick 2–3 gaps from above and bring candidate research questions tied to them.

Identified Research Gaps

  • Limited empirical work on clinician cognitive processes when using AI in real ED environments
  • Few rigorous interventions to mitigate automation bias in high-pressure clinical settings
  • Sparse literature on effects of AI on professional identity and meaning in emergency medicine specifically
  • Inadequate methods for evaluating human dimensions of AI (reasoning, wellbeing, patient communication)
  • Few longitudinal studies on trust dynamics and skill atrophy with sustained AI assistance
  • Limited guidance on governance and oversight of adaptive learning systems in clinical practice
  • Sparse research on patient perspectives and disclosure practices around AI in emergency care
  • Almost no ED-specific work on ambient AI scribes despite rapid deployment in ambulatory care

Meeting Facilitation Guides

Lead playbook for facilitating each virtual meeting. Visible only in the admin panel — WG4 members do not see these notes.

Meeting 1 — Kickoff & Scoping (Apr 22)

Meeting goal: Shared understanding of the charge, not consensus. Every member should leave knowing the scope, the process, and what they owe before Round 1 opens.

Tone-setting moves

  • Go round-robin, not open-floor, for introductions and the brainstorm. Early-career faculty, fellows, and non-physician members get crowded out otherwise.
  • Explicitly name that disagreement in Round 1 is expected and useful — it's exactly what Meeting 2 exists to resolve. This lowers the stakes and surfaces honest ratings.
  • No critique during the brainstorm. Capture everything first; refine later. Critique kills idea flow in the first 10 minutes.

Watch-outs

  • Don't let the 18-question walkthrough become a debate. This meeting is orientation, not deliberation.
  • If a senior voice starts anchoring ("I think we should obviously drop Q14…"), gently redirect: "Hold that — let's capture it as a comment in Round 1 so the group can weigh it anonymously."
  • Solo-facilitator tip: since you're running WG4 alone, use the chat as your second channel. Pin a running notes doc on your second monitor and glance at chat between agenda blocks rather than trying to watch it continuously while speaking.

Pre-reads to send ≥48 hours before

  • The candidate research questions and domain map
  • One-page Delphi process explainer (consensus threshold, rounds, what happens at each stage)
  • Link to the Meeting 1 page and Round 1 form (Round 1 opens post-meeting)

Post-meeting (within 24 hours)

  • Send recap with captured brainstorm items, Round 1 deadline, and the Discussion Doc link
  • Post the session recording (with consent) for anyone who missed
  • Unlock Round 1 survey in the admin panel
Meeting 2 — Round 1 Debrief & Refinement (Apr 29)

Meeting goal: Surface the reasoning behind divergent Round 1 ratings and refine wording before Round 2. Not re-litigating items that already hit consensus.

The critical move: steelman both sides before opening discussion

For each contested item, ask "What's the strongest argument for a high rating?" and "What's the strongest argument for a low rating?" before opening general discussion. This depersonalizes disagreement and almost always reveals that the issue is wording ambiguity, not substantive disagreement. It also prevents the first speaker from anchoring the room.

Tone-setting moves

  • Skip confirmed items entirely. Do not walk through questions that already hit ≥80%. State the number, show the tile, move on. This is where new facilitators burn 20 minutes.
  • Round-robin the first comment on each contested item, then open the floor. Prevents senior voices from anchoring.
  • For each contested item, force a decision before moving on: keep / reword / split / merge / drop. Don't leave ambiguity to resolve offline by yourself.

Watch-outs

  • Biggest risk: one or two senior members dominating the contested-item discussion. Use the round-robin as a structural defense.
  • Second risk: scope creep into re-discussing confirmed items. Gently redirect: "That's a great point — but Q7 already hit 85% Include, so let's keep moving."
  • If the group can't agree on rewording in the meeting, park the item, ask one member to partner with you on drafting options offline, and circulate before Round 2 opens.

Pre-reads to send 48 hours before

  • Round 1 distributions (include / include-with-modifications / exclude %)
  • All free-text comments grouped by item (not by respondent) so members can scan reasoning behind divergent ratings
  • A shortlist of the 4–8 items you plan to discuss — members know what's contested and can prepare

Post-meeting (within 24 hours)

  • Recap with decisions per contested item, updated question wording, and Round 2 open/close dates
  • Launch Round 2 form with revised wording; unlock Round 2 survey in the admin panel
Meeting 3 — Pre-Conference Alignment (May 6)

Meeting goal: Logistical and strategic, not deliberative. The group is fatigued — they need clarity on the day-of plan, not another deliberation. Keep it tight; end early if you can.

Tone-setting moves

  • Don't re-open Round 2 debates. If a member tries, acknowledge and redirect: "That's a great point to raise during the in-person breakout on May 21 — let's make sure you're assigned to speak to it." Voting is closed.
  • Assign roles explicitly, not voluntarily. "Voluntary" assignments don't stick. Name people: "Alex will present Domain A, Priya will scribe cross-group feedback, Jordan and I will captain the plenary vote tally."
  • Rehearse the hard questions. The audience will push on scope overlap with WGs 3 and 5, sample populations, and methodological rigor. Agree on language now so nobody freelances on stage.

Watch-outs

  • Fatigue is the dominant force at Meeting 3. Keep the energy up by being decisive. Long pauses and open "what do you all think?" questions drag the room down.
  • If attendance dips, that's OK — send absent members the recap, the role assignment, and a 15-min async ask to confirm.
  • Leave 2–3 minutes at the end for private last-minute concerns (offer "or grab me after the call if it's easier").

Pre-reads to send 48 hours before

  • Final Round 2 slate with CONFIRMED / PENDING status per item
  • Draft May 21 role assignments (so people can flex before the meeting)
  • Venue map, breakout room, timing, and what-to-bring list

Post-meeting (within 24 hours)

  • Send final role sheet, day-of logistics, and a one-page "WG4 slate" PDF each member can reference on May 21
  • Confirm the cross-group liaison handoff with WG3 and WG5 leads

Cross-cutting principles (all three meetings)

  • Start on time, end on time, send pre-reads ≥48 hours ahead
  • Running solo: record sessions (with consent) so you can review notes after rather than trying to capture everything live; consider recruiting one WG4 member to act as a designated scribe per meeting
  • Record sessions (with consent) so members who miss can stay engaged async
  • Send recap within 24 hours with decisions, action items, and the next deadline — momentum decays fast

Quick Reference

Recommended workflow:

1. Distribute the site with everything locked (default)

2. Hold the kickoff meeting, then unlock Round 1 Survey

3. After all Round 1 responses are in, lock Round 1 Survey and unlock Round 1 Results

4. Unlock Round 2 Survey; notify participants to complete it

5. After Round 2 closes, lock Round 2 Survey and unlock Round 2 Results

How lock state persists: toggling rewrites your browser URL to include query params like ?r1survey=open&roster=open. That URL is the access link — whatever you email to WG4 members is what determines their view. The base URL (no params) always defaults to locked.

Want the base URL itself to default to unlocked? That requires a code edit to getLockState() in index.html and a GitHub commit. For normal Delphi cadence, sharing a new link at each stage is fine.
